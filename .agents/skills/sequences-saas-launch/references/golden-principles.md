# SaaS launch craft bar

The legacy Slack Sequences golden demo is a quality reference, not an implementation scaffold. Its useful shape is a 28-second causal arc: visible friction, an energy-peak collapse into a product mark, typed interaction, a workspace superzoom, a reply caused by that interaction, proof cards, and a four-second resting lockup.

Reuse these principles:

- Contrast crowded and quiet moments. Constant density makes the film feel flat.
- Make the product action cause the next shot. Continuity matters more than decorative transition count.
- Preserve one recognizable object, color, shape, cursor, or content payload through major handoffs.
- Preserve continuity by changing the persistent product surface in place. Do not re-run its entrance, rebuild the same chrome, or flash an already-visible control back through its hidden state at a later beat.
- Let proof emerge from product state rather than replacing the product with generic claims.
- Stage one memorable energy peak and one confident final hold.
- Change editorial scale at least once. A wide establishing product view, an operated close-up, and a simplified pullback create more cinematic range than a dashboard held at one size.
- Make the energy peak visible in still samples: silhouette, spatial density, scale, or product state should transform. A cursor press or small status-color change alone is not a peak.
- Treat interface microcopy as texture unless it is large enough to read in motion. Carry each beat's retained idea with video-scale type or an unmistakable product-state change.
- Resolve by subtracting. The ending should reduce competing chrome and rest on brand, promise, and one proof object rather than placing a logo over the same busy frame.
- Use the prompt's actual product category and tone. The golden demo is not a palette, layout, or copy template.

Avoid its legacy mechanics: custom `window.__timeline` rendering, seek clamps, duplicated guard elements, giant camera runtimes, regex capability defaults, generic transition registries, and automatic injection of the same grain/shimmer/3D/logo-outro effects into every film.

## Human review questions

- Can a viewer name the product promise after one watch?
- Is there a credible product or UI action, not only typography?
- Does every major move land before the next idea starts?
- Does each persistent UI element enter exactly once, with later beats changing state rather than invoking it again?
- Is the transition grammar coherent, with at least one identity-preserving handoff where appropriate?
- Does the film have more dynamic range than a deck of animated cards?
- Is the last frame composed to rest rather than merely stopping?
